## How Aggregation Levels Changed My Understanding of Detection Rules

Throughout this phase, I realized the importance of the time aggregation level or time interval. When creating rules, it's common to use visual cues, such as spikes in the graph, to determine how to detect these activities. Initially, I chose a threshold based on spikes, but later I found that the threshold didn't work, or the alerts didn't visually align with the graphs. After conducting research, I learned that detection rules, especially threshold-based ones, should be derived from alert intent and statistical analysis, not just from visual intuition.

Before learning those, my original rules were simply something like "High Spike on Port 445 Activity". Then I created a rule, tested it with Rule Preview, and annotated it; it looked like it was working. But then, when I was looking at the graph with a different time interval, I noticed there were higher spikes than in my original spike. What confused me was that I was not thinking about the aggregation level and how it can change the shape of the graph.

The useful analogy for me was to think about "rain". Let's say the hourly graph shows there were 60mm of rain per hour. It may look like a spike in a graph. Now, if I go to a more granular time interval, I may see another spike-like activity, which may represent storm-like rain that occurred over a short period, or a not-so-spike-like but constant slope of a line, which may represent steady rain over a certain period of time. So, depending on the aggregation level, the graph changes shape and can be interpreted in different ways. This made me realize that a spike does not inherently represent the "intensity" of an activity. Depending on the aggregation level, a spike may reflect a short burst of activity, sustained activity accumulated over a longer period, or simply a larger overall volume of events. Therefore, the meaning of a spike depends on how the data is aggregated and what behavior the detection rule is intended to capture.  And it's important to define what I want to be alerted to first.

Going back to the rain analogy, I asked what I would be interested in being alerted to: e.g., checking whether the daily rain amount exceeded a certain volume? Checking how heavy it rained hourly during the day? From there, I would choose the observation period, e.g., 30 days or 7 days? What would be an appropriate time interval? "day" or "hour"? Then I would check a graph with such a scale, identify a baseline and an anomaly, and compute the threshold. Realizing this shifted how I approached detection engineering. Instead of asking, "Where is the spike and what threshold should I choose?", I started asking, "What behavior do I want to detect?" From there, I could select an appropriate observation period, determine the aggregation interval, establish a baseline from historical data, and derive thresholds aligned with the detection objective.  Now my rule became something like: "SMB Negotiate Command - 12h Volume Threshold Exceeded", more concise and easier to see what's being alerted. I think the intention was the same; I wanted to be alerted when a spike happens, but now it's clearer.
